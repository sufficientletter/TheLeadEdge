# Public Records & Life Event Data: High-Probability Real Estate Lead Strategies

## Overview

Public records are the single most underutilized data source in real estate lead generation. While most agents rely on expired MLS listings and cold-calling purchased lists, the agents who consistently outperform are mining county recorder offices, court filings, and municipal databases to identify motivated sellers **before** they list --- or before they even realize they need to sell.

This document covers 12 distinct public record categories, each representing a life event or financial signal that creates a high-probability real estate transaction. For each, we detail where to find the data, how to access it, what makes it a strong lead, legal guardrails, and suggested outreach approaches.

**Important**: All strategies in this document assume the Realtor is licensed and operating ethically. The goal is to provide helpful service to people in transition --- not to exploit vulnerable situations.

## 1. Pre-Foreclosure & Notice of Default (NOD)

### What It Is

A Notice of Default (NOD) is a public filing recorded when a homeowner falls significantly behind on mortgage payments --- typically 90+ days delinquent. This is the formal first step in the foreclosure process. In some states, the equivalent filing is a **Lis Pendens** (notice of pending legal action). The homeowner still has a redemption window to cure the default, and this is when a sale is most advantageous to them.

### Where to Find This Data

| Source | Access Method | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| **County Recorder / Clerk of Court** | In-person or online portal | Free to low cost |
| **PropertyRadar** | Subscription platform | ~$100/mo |
| **ATTOM Data (RealtyTrac)** | Subscription / API | Varies |
| **Foreclosure.com** | Online subscription | ~$40/mo |
| **Public Notice websites** (state-specific) | Online | Free |
| **County GIS/Parcel Viewer** | Often linked from county site | Free |

**Specific access steps:**
1. Search "[Your County] Recorder of Deeds" or "[Your County] Clerk of Court" online.
2. Most counties now have electronic recording systems with searchable indexes.
3. Search document type for "Notice of Default," "Lis Pendens," or "Notice of Trustee Sale."
4. Many counties allow free searches but charge $0.50--$2.00 per page to view the actual document.
5. Some states (California, Arizona, Nevada, Oregon, Washington) are "non-judicial foreclosure" states where NODs are recorded directly with the county recorder. Judicial foreclosure states (New York, New Jersey, Florida, Illinois) file through the court system.

### What Makes This a High-Probability Lead

- **Urgency**: The homeowner is on a legal clock. In most states, they have 90--120 days after the NOD to cure the default before a trustee sale is scheduled.
- **Motivation**: The homeowner stands to lose all their equity if the property goes to auction. A traditional sale preserves their equity and credit far better than a foreclosure.
- **Volume**: Pre-foreclosure filings are a steady, renewable source of leads. Even in strong markets, there are always homeowners who fall behind.
- **Equity opportunity**: Many pre-foreclosure homeowners have significant equity (especially those who bought or refinanced years ago) and simply need help navigating the sale process quickly.

### Timeline: Signal to Potential Listing

```
Day 0:        Mortgage payments stop
Day 90-120:   NOD/Lis Pendens filed (THIS IS YOUR SIGNAL)
Day 90-180:   Reinstatement period (homeowner can cure default)
Day 150-270:  Notice of Trustee Sale / Foreclosure Sale scheduled
Day 180-365:  Auction date
Day 365+:     REO (bank-owned) if not sold at auction
```

**Best outreach window**: Days 90--150 after the NOD filing. Early enough that the homeowner has options, late enough that they realize the urgency.

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

- **Federal**: The FTC's Mortgage Assistance Relief Services (MARS) Rule prohibits misleading claims about foreclosure rescue. Do not claim you can "stop" a foreclosure --- you are offering a market-rate sale as an alternative.
- **State laws**: Many states have specific statutes governing contact with homeowners in foreclosure. California (CC 2945), New York, and others restrict "foreclosure consultants." As a licensed Realtor offering to list the property at fair market value, you are generally exempt from these restrictions, but verify with your broker.
- **Do Not Call**: Pre-foreclosure homeowners may be on the DNC list. Direct mail is the safest outreach channel. If calling, scrub against the DNC registry first.
- **Equity purchase restrictions**: Some states require that if you (or an investor you represent) purchase a pre-foreclosure property, you must pay at least a certain percentage of fair market value (e.g., California requires 82%).
- **Sensitivity**: These homeowners are under extreme stress. Lead with empathy and service, not with urgency or fear-based messaging.

### Suggested Outreach Approach

**Channel**: Direct mail (primary), door knocking (secondary, if in your farm area)

**Messaging framework**:
- Acknowledge the situation without being invasive: "I noticed some changes on your property records and wanted to reach out..."
- Lead with value: "Many homeowners in this situation don't realize they may have options that protect their equity and credit."
- Offer a free, no-obligation consultation: Comparative Market Analysis (CMA) showing what the home could sell for.
- Provide a clear timeline: "Based on where things stand, you likely have [X weeks/months] to explore your options."
- **Do NOT** mention the word "foreclosure" on the exterior of any mail piece (this is illegal in many jurisdictions and always unethical).

**Drip sequence**: 3--5 touches over 60 days. Start with a letter, follow up with a postcard, then a second letter with a CMA offer.

**Conversion expectation**: 1--3% response rate on direct mail; 10--20% of responders convert to a listing within 6 months.

## 2. Probate & Inherited Properties

### What It Is

When a property owner dies, the property typically passes through probate --- a court-supervised process to settle the deceased's estate. The personal representative (executor/administrator) often needs to sell the property to distribute proceeds among heirs, pay estate debts, or simply because no heir wants to keep it. Inherited properties are one of the highest-probability lead sources because the heir often has no emotional attachment, lives out of area, and needs to liquidate.

### Where to Find This Data

| Source | Access Method | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| **County Probate Court** | Online docket search or in-person | Free |
| **County Recorder** | Death certificates, affidavits of heirship | Free--low cost |
| **State court electronic filing systems** | e.g., Odyssey (Tyler Technologies), PACER for federal | Free--$0.10/page |
| **Obituary databases** (Legacy.com, local newspapers) | Online | Free |
| **USProbateLeads.com** | Pre-compiled lead lists | ~$50--200/mo |
| **AllTheLeads.com** | Pre-compiled probate leads | ~$200--400/mo |
| **County tax assessor** | Check for ownership changes or unpaid taxes on a decedent's property | Free |

**Specific access steps:**
1. Search "[Your County] Probate Court" or "[Your County] Surrogate's Court."
2. Look for online case search / docket search portals.
3. Filter for new probate filings (often categorized as "Decedent's Estates," "Estate Administration," or "Intestate Estate").
4. The filing will name the personal representative and often list real property in the estate.
5. Cross-reference the personal representative's name with the county tax assessor to identify all properties in the estate.
6. For higher volume, subscribe to a service that compiles these filings across multiple counties.

### What Makes This a High-Probability Lead

- **High motivation**: 60--70% of inherited properties are sold within 12--24 months of the probate filing.
- **Out-of-area heirs**: Many heirs live in a different city or state and have zero interest in managing the property.
- **Emotional readiness**: After the initial grieving period, heirs are often eager to settle the estate and move forward.
- **Equity-rich**: These properties often have no mortgage or a very small remaining balance, making the sale straightforward.
- **Deferred maintenance**: Inherited properties frequently need work, and heirs don't want to invest in a property they plan to sell. They need an agent who can guide them through selling as-is or coordinating cost-effective repairs.

### Timeline: Signal to Potential Listing

```
Day 0:        Property owner passes away
Day 0-30:     Family/estate arrangements
Day 30-90:    Probate petition filed with court (YOUR SIGNAL)
Day 60-180:   Court appoints personal representative
Day 90-365:   Estate administration (debts settled, assets identified)
Day 180-540:  Property prepared for sale / listed
Day 365-730:  Estate closed
```

**Best outreach window**: 30--60 days after the probate filing. The personal representative has been appointed and is beginning to understand the scope of their responsibilities. Too early feels predatory; too late means another agent got there first.

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

- **Sensitivity is paramount**: Someone has died. Your outreach must be respectful and never mention the death directly in an exploitative way.
- **Probate court approval**: In many jurisdictions, the sale of probate property requires court confirmation. Familiarize yourself with the probate sale process in your state (e.g., California's "overbid" process).
- **Personal representative authority**: The PR may have full authority (under the Independent Administration of Estates Act, where applicable) or may need court approval for every sale decision. Understanding this distinction helps you guide the process.
- **HIPAA/Privacy**: Death records are public. Probate filings are public. You are not accessing private health information.

### Suggested Outreach Approach

**Channel**: Direct mail to the personal representative (primary), followed by phone call if number is available

**Messaging framework**:
- Open with condolence: "I'm sorry for the loss of [Decedent's name]."
- Establish expertise: "I specialize in helping families navigate the sale of inherited properties, including the probate process."
- Offer specific help: "I can provide a free property valuation and walk you through the timeline and requirements for a probate sale in [County]."
- Reduce overwhelm: "Settling an estate involves a lot of moving parts. I work closely with probate attorneys and can help coordinate the process."
- Include a personal touch: handwritten envelope, personal note, not a mass-produced mailer.

**Drip sequence**: Initial letter at 30--60 days post-filing. Follow-up letter at 90 days. Third touch at 120 days. These leads have a long cycle --- keep following up quarterly for up to 18 months.

**Conversion expectation**: 3--7% response rate; 15--25% of responders eventually list. Higher than most lead sources because of the inherent need to transact.

## 3. Divorce Filings

### What It Is

Divorce proceedings frequently require the division or liquidation of marital assets, and the family home is typically the largest single asset. Courts often order the sale of the marital home as part of the divorce settlement, or one spouse needs to sell because they can no longer afford the mortgage alone. Divorce filings are public records in every state.

### Where to Find This Data

| Source | Access Method | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| **County Family Court / Superior Court** | Online docket search | Free |
| **State court electronic filing systems** | e.g., Odyssey, eCourt | Free |
| **Courthouse physical records** | In-person search | Free |
| **Newspaper legal notices** | Divorce summons published in newspapers of record | Free |
| **Third-party lead providers** | Services that compile divorce filings | ~$100--300/mo |

**Specific access steps:**
1. Search "[Your County] Family Court records" or "[Your County] Superior Court civil filings."
2. Filter by case type: "Dissolution of Marriage," "Divorce," or "Family Law."
3. The initial petition is public; however, financial details are usually sealed.
4. Cross-reference the petitioner/respondent names with county property records to identify if they own real property.
5. Focus on filings where at least one party owns property in your target area.

### What Makes This a High-Probability Lead

- **Forced transaction**: Unlike most life events, divorce often legally requires a property sale. A court may order the sale, or the financial reality of supporting two households demands it.
- **Urgency**: Both parties typically want the process over quickly. Speed of sale is often prioritized over maximizing price.
- **Dual opportunity**: Divorce creates both a listing opportunity (selling the marital home) and buyer opportunities (one or both parties need new housing).
- **Reliable volume**: Divorce rates remain steady regardless of market conditions, providing a consistent lead pipeline.
- **Equity access**: Selling the home is often the only way for both parties to access their share of equity.

### Timeline: Signal to Potential Listing

```
Day 0:        Divorce petition filed (YOUR SIGNAL)
Day 0-90:     Temporary orders / mediation
Day 90-180:   Discovery and negotiation of asset division
Day 120-270:  Settlement agreement or trial
Day 180-365:  Final decree / judgment
Day 180-540:  Property sale executed (if ordered)
```

**Best outreach window**: 60--120 days after the initial filing. The emotional shock has settled, and practical decisions about assets are being made. Too early feels intrusive.

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

- **Extreme sensitivity**: Divorce is deeply personal. Your marketing must never reference the divorce directly on any exterior mail piece.
- **Both parties**: In most cases, both spouses must agree to the sale terms. Your outreach should be to both parties if both are on title, though practically you may only reach one.
- **Attorney involvement**: Many divorce sales involve attorneys. Be prepared to work with legal counsel on both sides.
- **Court-ordered sales**: If the court orders a sale, the listing price and terms may be subject to court approval. Familiarize yourself with this process.
- **Do Not Contact orders**: If either party has a restraining order or requests no contact, respect it immediately.

### Suggested Outreach Approach

**Channel**: Direct mail (strongly preferred). Phone and door knocking are too invasive for this life event.

**Messaging framework**:
- Do NOT mention divorce. Frame around "life transitions" or "changes in housing needs."
- "If you're considering a change in your living situation, I'd be happy to provide a confidential market analysis of your home."
- Emphasize confidentiality and discretion.
- Highlight your ability to facilitate a smooth, fast sale.
- Offer a free CMA with no obligation.
- Position yourself as a resource, not a salesperson.

**Drip sequence**: 2--3 touches over 90 days. Gentle, non-aggressive. A single tasteful letter followed by one postcard.

**Conversion expectation**: 1--2% response rate; 15--20% of responders list within 12 months. Lower response rate than probate because many couples resolve without selling, but high conversion among those who do respond.

## 4. Tax Delinquency

### What It Is

When property owners fail to pay their property taxes, the county records a tax lien against the property. After a period of delinquency (varies by state, typically 1--5 years), the county can sell the property at a tax lien auction or tax deed sale. Owners with delinquent taxes are often financially distressed and may be motivated to sell before losing the property entirely.

### Where to Find This Data

| Source | Access Method | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| **County Tax Assessor / Treasurer** | Online portal (most counties) | Free |
| **County tax sale / auction lists** | Published before auctions, often online | Free |
| **State Department of Revenue** | State-level delinquent tax databases | Free |
| **GIS / Parcel mapping tools** | Cross-reference tax status with property data | Free |
| **ATTOM Data / CoreLogic** | Aggregated tax delinquency data | Subscription |
| **PropertyShark / PropertyRadar** | Tax lien status filters | Subscription |

**Specific access steps:**
1. Go to your county tax assessor or treasurer website.
2. Many counties publish a "delinquent tax list" annually, often before the tax sale. This is a public document.
3. Search by parcel number, owner name, or address to check individual property tax status.
4. Look for properties that are 1+ years delinquent --- these owners are most motivated.
5. Cross-reference with county recorder data to confirm ownership and check for additional liens.
6. Many counties mail the delinquent list to anyone who requests it, or publish it in the local newspaper of record.

### What Makes This a High-Probability Lead

- **Financial distress signal**: Failing to pay property taxes is one of the strongest indicators of financial hardship or property abandonment.
- **Loss of property at stake**: The owner faces losing the property entirely if taxes aren't cured. A sale allows them to extract equity.
- **Overlapping distress**: Tax delinquency often overlaps with other distress signals (mortgage default, code violations, vacancy), amplifying the probability of a sale.
- **Equity extraction**: Even in a tax-delinquent situation, the homeowner may have substantial equity that a sale would unlock.
- **Pre-auction urgency**: As the auction date approaches, motivation increases dramatically.

### Timeline: Signal to Potential Listing

```
Year 0:       Property taxes become delinquent
Year 0-1:     Penalties and interest accrue
Year 1-2:     County sends notices, publishes delinquent list
Year 2-3:     Tax lien certificate issued (in lien states)
Year 3-5:     Tax deed sale / auction scheduled
```

**Best outreach window**: 6--18 months before the scheduled tax sale. The owner has had time to realize the problem but still has time to sell conventionally.

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

- **Tax sale redemption periods**: Most states allow the owner to redeem (pay back taxes + penalties) even after the sale. Understand your state's redemption rights.
- **Equity theft concerns**: Recent legal challenges and legislation (Tyler v. Hennepin County, U.S. Supreme Court 2023) have addressed "home equity theft" where counties keep surplus proceeds from tax sales. This is changing the landscape --- be aware of your state's laws.
- **Do not misrepresent urgency**: Provide accurate timelines. Do not tell an owner they will "lose their home tomorrow" if the auction is 18 months away.
- **Investor partnerships**: If connecting distressed owners with investors, ensure the owner understands the transaction and receives fair value.

### Suggested Outreach Approach

**Channel**: Direct mail (primary), phone (secondary, after DNC scrub)

**Messaging framework**:
- "County records indicate there may be outstanding obligations on your property. I help homeowners explore their options, including selling at full market value."
- Offer a free property valuation.
- Explain that selling before a tax sale preserves their equity and credit.
- Provide a clear, honest timeline of what happens if taxes aren't resolved.
- Never use scare tactics --- present facts and options.

**Drip sequence**: Monthly mailers starting 18 months before the projected sale date. Increase frequency to bi-weekly in the final 6 months.

**Conversion expectation**: 2--4% response rate; variable conversion depending on equity position. Highest conversion when combined with other distress signals.

## 5. Code Violations & Condemned Properties

### What It Is

Municipal code enforcement departments issue violations for properties that don't meet local building, housing, or safety codes. These range from minor issues (overgrown lawn, peeling paint) to major structural problems (condemned buildings, uninhabitable conditions). Code violations are public records and signal an owner who is either unable or unwilling to maintain the property --- both of which indicate potential motivation to sell.

### Where to Find This Data

| Source | Access Method | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| **City/County Code Enforcement Department** | Online portal or records request | Free |
| **Municipal court records** | Code violation cases are adjudicated in municipal court | Free |
| **Building Department / Permit Office** | Expired permits, stop-work orders | Free |
| **City open data portals** | Many cities publish code violation data (e.g., city data portals using Socrata/Tyler) | Free |
| **311 complaint databases** | Some cities publish 311 data, including property complaints | Free |
| **Fire Marshal records** | Fire code violations, especially on multi-family | Free |

**Specific access steps:**
1. Search "[Your City] code enforcement" or "[Your City] code violations."
2. Many municipalities have searchable online databases (e.g., using Accela, CityView, or custom portals).
3. Search by address to see open and historical violations.
4. Focus on properties with: (a) multiple open violations, (b) violations older than 6 months (unresolved), (c) condemnation or "unfit for habitation" notices.
5. File a public records request (FOIA/state equivalent) if the data is not online. Most cities will provide a spreadsheet of open violations.
6. Check the building permit office for permits that were pulled but never finaled (expired permits), or for stop-work orders. These indicate stalled renovation projects.

### What Makes This a High-Probability Lead

- **Overwhelmed owner**: Multiple or severe code violations often indicate an owner who is financially or physically unable to maintain the property.
- **Mounting fines**: Code violation fines accumulate daily in many jurisdictions ($50--$500/day is common). The financial pressure to resolve the situation grows rapidly.
- **Condemnation risk**: If the property is condemned, the owner cannot rent it or live in it, creating urgent motivation to sell.
- **Below-market opportunity**: Properties with code violations often sell below market value, creating opportunity for investor-buyers (and you can represent both sides or connect sellers with your investor network).
- **Stalled renovation = distressed flipper**: Expired permits and stop-work orders often indicate a flipper or renovator who ran out of money or encountered unexpected problems. These owners are highly motivated.

### Timeline: Signal to Potential Listing

```
Day 0:        Code violation issued
Day 30-90:    Compliance deadline
Day 90-180:   Fines begin accruing / case referred to municipal court
Day 180-365:  Liens placed on property for unpaid fines
Day 365+:     Property may be condemned or scheduled for demolition
```

**Best outreach window**: 60--180 days after the initial violation, especially if the violation remains unresolved. The owner is feeling the pressure but hasn't yet been forced into the worst outcomes.

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

- **Code violation records are public**: Accessing and using this information is legal in all U.S. jurisdictions.
- **Do not trespass**: While researching properties, do not enter private property to assess condition.
- **Honest representation**: If the property has serious issues, be upfront with potential buyers about the scope of repairs needed.
- **Investor ethics**: If connecting the seller with an investor, ensure the seller understands fair market value versus the investor's offer.

### Suggested Outreach Approach

**Channel**: Direct mail, door knocking (if property appears occupied and safe), or phone

**Messaging framework**:
- "I work with homeowners dealing with property challenges. Whether you're looking to sell as-is or need help understanding your options, I'm here to help."
- Emphasize your network: investors who buy as-is, contractors who can provide repair estimates, attorneys who handle code compliance.
- Offer a no-obligation property assessment.
- For condemned properties: "I have experience helping owners sell properties in any condition. There are buyers for every property."

**Conversion expectation**: 3--8% response rate. Code violation leads have a high conversion rate because the owner is often facing financial penalties with no other clear solution.

## 6. Absentee Owners

### What It Is

An absentee owner is anyone who owns a property but does not live at that property --- their mailing address on file with the county tax assessor is different from the property address. This includes landlords, owners of second homes, inherited properties not yet sold, relocated owners who haven't sold, and vacant property owners. Absentee owners are one of the highest-volume and most reliable lead sources in real estate.

### Where to Find This Data

| Source | Access Method | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| **County Tax Assessor / Property Appraiser** | Online parcel search --- compare site address to mailing address | Free |
| **ListSource (CoreLogic)** | Bulk absentee owner lists with filtering | ~$0.05--0.15/record |
| **PropertyRadar** | Absentee owner filter | Subscription |
| **BatchLeads / PropStream** | Absentee owner filter with skip tracing | Subscription |
| **USPS National Change of Address (NCOA)** | Cross-reference with property records | Via direct mail providers |
| **County GIS / Parcel Viewer** | Manual search | Free |

**Specific access steps:**
1. Go to your county tax assessor / property appraiser website.
2. Search any property and compare the **site address** (where the property is) with the **mailing address** (where the tax bill is sent).
3. If they differ, the owner is absentee.
4. For bulk identification, use ListSource or a similar data provider. You can filter by: out-of-state owners, out-of-county owners, mailing address in a different ZIP code, etc.
5. For maximum impact, layer absentee ownership with other signals: absentee + tax delinquent, absentee + code violation, absentee + long-term ownership.

### What Makes This a High-Probability Lead

- **Distance = difficulty**: Managing a property from a distance is inconvenient, costly, and stressful. Many absentee owners would sell if approached with the right offer.
- **Tenant problems**: Landlords dealing with difficult tenants, vacancies, or rising maintenance costs are often ready to exit.
- **Life change completion**: Many absentee owners relocated for a job but kept the property "just in case." After 1--3 years, they're ready to cut ties.
- **Estate/inheritance**: Inherited properties where the heir lives elsewhere are high-motivation because the heir has no use for the property.
- **Massive volume**: In any given market, 20--40% of residential properties are absentee-owned, providing a large prospect pool to filter and prioritize.

### Targeting & Prioritization

Not all absentee owners are equal. Prioritize:

| Priority | Filter | Why |
|----------|--------|-----|
| **Highest** | Out-of-state + owned 2+ years + single-family | Maximum inconvenience, likely past the "holding period" |
| **High** | Absentee + tax delinquent | Financial distress + distance |
| **High** | Absentee + code violation | Maintenance problems they can't easily fix from afar |
| **Medium** | Out-of-county, same state | Less distance but still inconvenient |
| **Medium** | Absentee + free-and-clear (no mortgage) | High equity, may have forgotten about the property |
| **Lower** | In-county absentee | May be active landlords who intend to hold |

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

- **All data used is public**: Tax assessor records, including owner name and mailing address, are public information.
- **Do Not Call compliance**: If calling absentee owners, scrub numbers against the DNC registry. Many investors/landlords are on it.
- **Landlord-tenant issues**: If the property is occupied by tenants, do not contact the tenants about a potential sale. Deal directly with the owner.
- **Anti-solicitation ordinances**: Some municipalities restrict door-to-door solicitation. This mostly matters for occupied properties.

### Suggested Outreach Approach

**Channel**: Direct mail to the mailing address (not the property address), phone, or a combination

**Messaging framework**:
- "I specialize in helping property owners who don't live at the property they own. Whether you're a landlord considering cashing out or you've been holding a property you no longer need, I can help."
- Lead with market data: "Properties in [neighborhood] have appreciated X% in the last Y years. Your property at [address] may be worth more than you think."
- Offer a free, no-obligation property valuation.
- For investor-landlords, speak their language: ROI, cap rate, 1031 exchange opportunities, portfolio optimization.
- For inherited/relocated owners: emphasize convenience and hassle-free process.

**Drip sequence**: Monthly mailers for 6--12 months. Absentee owners are a long-term nurture play. Many won't respond to the first several touches but will eventually reach a decision point.

**Conversion expectation**: 0.5--2% response rate (lower because the pool is large and many are content to hold); 20--30% of responders convert to a listing. Volume makes up for the lower response rate.

## 7. Long-Term Ownership

### What It Is

Properties owned by the same person for 10, 15, 20+ years represent enormous untapped equity and potential life-stage transitions. Long-term owners may be empty nesters whose children have left, retirees considering downsizing, aging owners who can no longer maintain the property, or owners sitting on massive equity gains who don't realize how much their home is worth.

### Where to Find This Data

| Source | Access Method | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| **County Tax Assessor** | Ownership history, deed recording date | Free |
| **County Recorder** | Deed transfer history | Free |
| **ListSource / CoreLogic** | Filter by length of ownership | ~$0.05--0.15/record |
| **PropStream / BatchLeads** | Length of residency filter | Subscription |
| **MLS (via the Realtor's access)** | Properties NOT in MLS that match long-term ownership criteria | Free with access |

**Specific access steps:**
1. County tax assessor websites show when the current owner was recorded (deed date or "sale date").
2. Calculate years of ownership from the current date.
3. Filter for 10+, 15+, or 20+ years of ownership.
4. For bulk lists, use ListSource or PropStream and filter: ownership length > 10 years, owner-occupied, single-family residence.
5. Cross-reference with estimated equity (using tax assessed value vs. any recorded mortgages) to identify high-equity targets.

### What Makes This a High-Probability Lead

- **Life-stage transitions**: Owners in their 60s--70s who bought in their 30s--40s are approaching retirement, downsizing, or health-related moves. These are natural triggers.
- **Equity rich**: A homeowner who bought 15--20 years ago, especially pre-2010, likely has 50--100%+ equity. They have the financial flexibility to sell.
- **Under-improved**: Many long-term owners haven't updated their homes significantly. They may be reluctant to invest in updates but would benefit from selling to someone who will.
- **Not market-aware**: Many long-term owners have no idea what their home is worth in today's market. A well-prepared CMA showing their equity position can be the catalyst for a decision.
- **Estate planning**: Older long-term owners are thinking about estate planning and may prefer to sell now and distribute proceeds rather than leave the property to be probated.

### Targeting & Prioritization

| Priority | Filter | Signal |
|----------|--------|--------|
| **Highest** | 20+ year ownership + age 65+ + owner-occupied | Empty nester/retiree, likely downsizing |
| **High** | 15+ year ownership + free-and-clear | Maximum equity, no mortgage holding them |
| **High** | 10+ year ownership + large home (3000+ sqft) + 65+ | Oversized home for current needs |
| **Medium** | 10+ year ownership + high-appreciation area | May not realize equity gains |
| **Medium** | 15+ year ownership + low assessed value vs. comps | Under-assessed, big surprise CMA |

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

- **No distress element**: Unlike foreclosure or tax delinquency, long-term ownership is not a distress signal. Your outreach should not imply urgency or pressure.
- **Age-related sensitivity**: Older homeowners are protected by various elder abuse and predatory lending statutes. Never pressure, mislead, or take advantage of cognitive decline.
- **Fair housing**: Your marketing to long-term owners must not target or exclude based on protected classes (race, national origin, familial status, etc.).

### Suggested Outreach Approach

**Channel**: Direct mail (primary), community events, door knocking in farm area

**Messaging framework**:
- Lead with information, not solicitation: "Did you know that homes in [neighborhood] have increased in value by X% since [year they purchased]? Your home at [address] could be worth [estimated value range]."
- "Whether you're thinking about downsizing, relocating, or simply curious about your home's value, I'd love to provide a complimentary market analysis."
- Focus on their next chapter: "I help homeowners transition to the next stage --- whether that's a smaller home, a retirement community, or moving closer to family."
- Emphasize your track record with similar clients.

**Drip sequence**: Quarterly mailers with market updates. This is a "farm and nurture" strategy. The conversion timeline is 6--24 months, but the listing values are typically high.

**Conversion expectation**: 0.5--1.5% response rate; 10--15% of responders list within 18 months. Low response but high average sale price makes this very profitable per conversion.

## 8. Estate & Trust Transfers

### What It Is

Properties held in living trusts, family trusts, or transferred through estate planning instruments represent a distinct lead category. Trust transfers often happen when the trust creator (trustor/grantor) passes away and the successor trustee takes over, or when trust terms are amended (often signaling a life change). Unlike probate, trust transfers can happen without court involvement, but they still create public records when deeds are recorded.

### Where to Find This Data

| Source | Access Method | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| **County Recorder** | Deed transfers showing trust as grantor or grantee | Free--low cost |
| **County Tax Assessor** | Ownership changes where new owner is a trust | Free |
| **Title company records** | Title officers can identify trust-held properties | Relationship-based |
| **Affidavits of Death of Trustee** | Recorded with county when a trustor dies | Free |
| **Trust Transfer Deeds** | Deed from individual to trust or trust to individual | Free |

**Specific access steps:**
1. At the county recorder website, search recent deed recordings for:
   - Deeds where the grantor or grantee includes "Trust," "Trustee," "Family Trust," "Living Trust," or "Revocable Trust."
   - "Affidavit of Death of Trustee" or "Affidavit --- Change of Trustee."
   - "Certificate of Trust" recordings.
2. Focus on recent recordings (last 30--90 days) for the freshest leads.
3. Deeds transferring property OUT of a trust (trust to individual) often precede a sale.
4. Deeds transferring property INTO a trust may indicate estate planning --- a longer-term nurture lead.

### What Makes This a High-Probability Lead

- **Trustor death = liquidation trigger**: When the trust creator dies, the successor trustee's primary job is to distribute assets. Selling real property is the most common way to do this.
- **Bypasses probate delays**: Trust properties can be sold faster than probate properties because there's no court oversight required (in most cases). This means the timeline from death to listing can be very short.
- **Similar profile to probate**: Successor trustees are often out-of-area, unfamiliar with the local market, and eager to complete their fiduciary duties.
- **Trust-to-individual transfers**: When property is deeded out of a trust to an individual (often an heir), that individual frequently sells shortly after --- they received the property as a distribution, not by choice.
- **Multiple properties**: Trusts with real estate portfolios may need to liquidate several properties, creating multi-listing opportunities.

### Timeline: Signal to Potential Listing

```
Day 0:        Trustor passes away
Day 0-30:     Successor trustee assumes control
Day 15-60:    Affidavit of Death of Trustee recorded (YOUR SIGNAL)
Day 30-90:    Trust administration begins
Day 60-180:   Property valued, debts settled
Day 90-365:   Property sold or distributed to beneficiaries
Day 90-540:   If distributed, beneficiary may sell shortly after
```

**Best outreach window**: Within 30--60 days of the affidavit or deed recording. Trust administrations move faster than probate.

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

- **Trust documents are private**: Unlike probate filings, the actual trust document is not public. You can only see what's recorded with the county (deeds, affidavits).
- **Fiduciary duty**: The successor trustee has a fiduciary obligation to the beneficiaries. They must sell at fair market value. Position yourself as helping them fulfill this duty.
- **Same sensitivity as probate**: A death has occurred. Approach with respect and empathy.

### Suggested Outreach Approach

**Channel**: Direct mail to the successor trustee, phone

**Messaging framework**: Similar to probate, but emphasize the streamlined process:
- "I understand you may be managing trust responsibilities. I specialize in helping trustees efficiently handle real property sales."
- "Trust property sales can move quickly since court confirmation is typically not required. I can help you get fair market value with minimal hassle."
- Offer to connect them with estate attorneys and CPAs if needed.

**Conversion expectation**: Similar to probate. 3--5% response rate with strong conversion among responders.

## 9. Building Permit Activity

### What It Is

Building permit data reveals two powerful signals: **(1) neighborhood momentum** --- areas where heavy renovation activity indicates rising values, and **(2) distressed renovators** --- properties where permits were pulled but never completed (expired permits, stop-work orders). Both create actionable lead opportunities.

### Where to Find This Data

| Source | Access Method | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| **City/County Building Department** | Online permit portal | Free |
| **City open data portals** | Bulk permit data downloads | Free |
| **BuildZoom** | Aggregated permit data across jurisdictions | Free tier available |
| **Zillow / Redfin** | Permit history on property pages (limited) | Free |
| **Construction monitoring services** | Dodge Data, Construction Monitor | Subscription |

**Specific access steps:**
1. Search "[Your City] building permits" or "[Your City] permit portal."
2. Most cities use platforms like Accela, CityView, or eTRAKiT for permit management with public-facing search.
3. Search for recent permits by address, date range, or permit type.
4. For **neighborhood momentum**: Aggregate permits by neighborhood/ZIP code. Look for areas with 20%+ increase in permit activity (especially renovation and addition permits) compared to prior years.
5. For **distressed renovators**: Search for permits with status "Expired," "Suspended," or "Revoked." Also look for permits pulled 12+ months ago that were never finaled.
6. Download bulk data if available (many cities offer CSV/Excel downloads of all permits).

### What Makes This a High-Probability Lead

**Neighborhood momentum signals:**
- Heavy renovation activity = rising property values = equity gains for existing owners.
- New construction permits indicate developer confidence in the area.
- Renovation clustering often precedes a wave of sales as "original" owners cash in on rising values.
- These neighborhoods are where your buyer-clients should be looking, too.

**Expired/stalled permit signals:**
- The owner/flipper ran out of money, time, or motivation.
- An incomplete renovation makes the property hard to sell conventionally --- they may need a creative solution.
- Stop-work orders indicate code compliance issues on top of the stalled work.
- These properties are often sold at a discount to investors or experienced buyers.

### Timeline

**Neighborhood momentum**: Ongoing monitoring. Set up monthly reviews of permit activity by area.

**Expired permits**:
```
Day 0:        Permit pulled (renovation begins)
Month 6-12:   Permit nearing expiration
Month 12-18:  Permit expires (YOUR SIGNAL)
Month 12-24:  Owner decides to sell or re-permit
```

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

- **Permit data is public**: Building permits are public records in every jurisdiction.
- **Trespass**: Do not enter properties to inspect the status of permitted work.
- **Buyer protection**: If selling a property with expired permits or incomplete work, full disclosure is required.

### Suggested Outreach Approach

**For expired permits:**
- Direct mail: "I noticed a building project at [address] may need some attention. Whether you're looking to complete the work, sell as-is, or explore other options, I can help."
- Offer connections to contractors and investors.

**For neighborhood momentum (listing generation):**
- Farm the neighborhood with market data: "Your neighborhood is one of the hottest renovation areas in [City]. Home values have increased X% in the last Y months. Curious what your home is worth?"
- This is a fantastic farming strategy that combines permit data with MLS data.

**Conversion expectation**: Expired permit leads: 3--5% response rate with moderate conversion. Neighborhood farming: long-term play with 0.5--1% response rate building over time.

## 10. Property Tax Assessment Changes

### What It Is

When a county reassesses a property and significantly increases its assessed value, the owner faces a higher property tax bill --- sometimes dramatically higher. This can be a shock, especially for owners on fixed incomes, and can trigger a decision to sell. Conversely, properties that receive a reduced assessment may signal declining condition or neighborhood issues.

### Where to Find This Data

| Source | Access Method | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| **County Tax Assessor** | Online search --- compare current vs. prior year assessed values | Free |
| **County assessment roll** | Published annually, often downloadable | Free |
| **Property tax appeal filings** | Public records showing owners contesting assessments | Free |
| **State equalization board** | Statewide assessment data | Free |
| **Data providers (ATTOM, CoreLogic)** | Historical assessment data with change calculations | Subscription |

**Specific access steps:**
1. County tax assessor websites show assessed value history for each parcel.
2. Download the annual assessment roll (if available) and calculate year-over-year changes.
3. Flag properties with 20%+ assessment increases.
4. Also flag properties where owners have filed tax appeals --- these owners are feeling the financial pinch.
5. Cross-reference large assessment increases with owner demographics (age, length of ownership) for highest-impact outreach.

### What Makes This a High-Probability Lead

- **Fixed-income impact**: Retirees and others on fixed incomes are disproportionately affected by tax increases. A 30% assessment increase translating to $2,000--5,000/year in additional taxes can make the home unaffordable.
- **Proposition 13 states**: In states like California, property tax is based on purchase price and only increases 2% annually --- until a reassessment event (new construction, change of ownership). New owners can face sudden, massive tax increases.
- **Sticker shock = motivation**: Owners who suddenly learn their taxes will double often begin considering whether it's time to sell.
- **Tax appeal filers**: Owners who file tax appeals are actively engaged with their property's financial reality and may be open to a conversation about selling.

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

- **Assessment data is public**: Tax rolls and assessment data are public records.
- **Do not provide tax advice**: You are a Realtor, not a CPA or tax attorney. Suggest they consult a professional for tax-specific questions.
- **No fear-mongering**: Present accurate information about assessment changes without exaggerating the impact.

### Suggested Outreach Approach

**Channel**: Direct mail, phone (after DNC scrub)

**Messaging framework**:
- "The county recently reassessed properties in [neighborhood], and many homeowners are seeing significant increases. I wanted to reach out with some information about your options."
- "If rising property taxes are making you rethink your housing situation, I can provide a free market analysis to show you what your equity position looks like."
- For tax appeal filers: "I noticed you recently filed a property tax appeal. That tells me you're paying close attention to your property's value. Would you like a free, independent market analysis?"

**Conversion expectation**: 1--2% response rate; moderate conversion. Works best when combined with long-term ownership and demographic filters.

## 11. Utility Disconnection Records

### What It Is

When utilities (water, electric, gas) are disconnected at a property, it strongly suggests the property is vacant. Vacant properties are among the most motivated seller situations because the owner is paying taxes, insurance, and potentially a mortgage on a property generating no income and providing no housing.

### Where to Find This Data

| Source | Access Method | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| **Municipal water/sewer department** | Public records request for disconnections | Free (FOIA) |
| **City open data portals** | Some cities publish utility data | Free |
| **USPS vacancy data** | Properties flagged as "vacant" in USPS database | Via data providers |
| **Property condition reports** | Code enforcement intersection | Free |
| **Driving for dollars** | Visual inspection of properties showing vacancy signs | Free (your time) |
| **Water meter reading data** | Some municipalities publish; zero-usage = vacant | Free (FOIA) |

**Specific access steps:**
1. File a public records request with your municipal water department for a list of properties where water service has been disconnected or where usage has been zero for 3+ months.
2. Not all municipalities will provide this, but many will under FOIA/state open records laws.
3. Cross-reference utility disconnection data with property ownership records to identify the owner.
4. Check USPS data through services like Melissa Data or SmartyStreets, which can flag addresses with "vacant" or "no-stat" designations.
5. Supplement with physical observation: overgrown lawns, mail accumulation, boarded windows, no vehicles.

### What Makes This a High-Probability Lead

- **Vacancy is costly**: The owner is paying carrying costs (taxes, insurance, HOA, mortgage) on a non-productive asset.
- **Deterioration risk**: Vacant properties deteriorate quickly, reducing value with each passing month. Pipes freeze, roofs leak, vandalism occurs.
- **Municipal pressure**: Many cities have vacant property registration ordinances that impose fees ($100--$5,000/year) and inspection requirements on vacant properties.
- **Insurance complications**: Many standard homeowner's policies don't cover vacant properties beyond 30--60 days. The owner may be exposed to liability.
- **Neighborhood nuisance**: Vacant properties attract complaints from neighbors, which can lead to code violations and fines (see Section 5).

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

- **Utility data access varies**: Some municipalities consider utility account data private. Your FOIA request may be partially or fully denied. This is jurisdiction-specific.
- **Trespass**: Never enter a vacant property without the owner's permission.
- **Squatter issues**: In some markets, vacant properties attract unauthorized occupants. Be aware of local squatter/adverse possession laws.
- **Honest condition assessment**: When valuing a vacant property, account for potential hidden damage and recommend inspections.

### Suggested Outreach Approach

**Channel**: Direct mail to the owner's mailing address (remember, they don't live at the property --- find their mailing address through tax records)

**Messaging framework**:
- "I noticed your property at [address] may not be occupied at this time. Vacant properties can be costly to maintain and may lose value over time."
- "I help property owners explore their options --- whether that's finding a tenant, selling at market value, or connecting with an investor who will purchase as-is."
- Emphasize the cost of holding: "Every month a property sits vacant, the owner faces taxes, insurance, potential fines, and maintenance costs with no return."
- Offer a free valuation and list of options.

**Conversion expectation**: 2--5% response rate. Vacant property owners who respond are often very motivated --- conversion rates among responders can exceed 30%.

## 12. Corporate-Owned Residential

### What It Is

Residential properties owned by LLCs, corporations, or other business entities represent a unique lead source. These may be investor-owned rentals, corporate-relocation properties, bank-owned assets, or portfolio holdings. When corporations liquidate residential assets, they often need a local agent to facilitate the sale, and the transactions tend to be straightforward (no emotional attachment, purely financial decisions).

### Where to Find This Data

| Source | Access Method | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| **County Tax Assessor** | Owner name search --- filter for LLC, Corp, Inc., Trust | Free |
| **Secretary of State business filings** | Look up LLC/Corp registered agent and officers | Free |
| **County Recorder** | Deeds where grantee is an entity, not an individual | Free |
| **ListSource / CoreLogic** | Filter by owner type: "Corporate" or "Non-Individual" | ~$0.05--0.15/record |
| **PropertyRadar / PropStream** | Entity-owned property filters | Subscription |
| **Bankruptcy court (PACER)** | Corporate bankruptcy filings listing real estate assets | $0.10/page |

**Specific access steps:**
1. On the county tax assessor website, search for owners with "LLC," "Inc.," "Corp," "LP," or "Holdings" in the name.
2. Look up the entity on your state's Secretary of State website to identify the registered agent, members, or officers.
3. For corporate bankruptcy liquidations, search PACER (Public Access to Court Electronic Records) for recent business bankruptcy filings in your district and look for real property in the asset schedules.
4. Monitor for multi-property LLC owners who begin selling --- if they sell one, they may be liquidating the portfolio.
5. REO (Real Estate Owned) departments at banks and credit unions regularly need local listing agents. Build relationships with asset managers.

### What Makes This a High-Probability Lead

- **No emotional attachment**: Corporations make financial decisions. If the numbers make sense to sell, they will.
- **Portfolio liquidation events**: When a small investor or LLC begins selling properties, there's often a triggering event (retirement, partnership dissolution, better opportunity elsewhere) that creates urgency to liquidate multiple properties.
- **Tax-motivated timing**: Corporate entities are highly aware of tax implications and may sell before year-end or at specific strategic times.
- **REO/Bank-owned**: Financial institutions are highly motivated sellers --- they don't want to hold residential property. REO listings tend to be straightforward transactions.
- **Corporate relocation**: Companies that purchase homes for relocating employees always need to sell those properties within 6--12 months.
- **Multi-listing potential**: One corporate contact can lead to 5, 10, or 50+ listings.

### Legal & Ethical Considerations

- **Entity identification**: LLC ownership can be used to maintain privacy. Respect the intent while using publicly available information.
- **Beneficial ownership**: Federal beneficial ownership reporting requirements (Corporate Transparency Act) may provide additional data on who controls LLCs, but this data has limited public access.
- **Commercial vs. residential regulations**: Even though the owner is an entity, if the property is residential, all residential real estate regulations apply (Fair Housing, disclosure requirements, etc.).

### Suggested Outreach Approach

**Channel**: Direct mail to registered agent, phone to entity principal, LinkedIn outreach, in-person for local investors

**Messaging framework**:
- Professional and business-focused: "I work with property investors and corporate owners to maximize returns on their residential holdings."
- Offer portfolio analysis: "I can provide a complimentary portfolio valuation for all your properties in [County/Area], including current market analysis and net proceeds estimates."
- Speak their language: cap rates, cash-on-cash returns, 1031 exchanges, portfolio optimization.
- For REO: Contact bank asset managers directly and offer your listing services. Provide your track record, BPO (Broker Price Opinion) experience, and asset preservation capabilities.

**Conversion expectation**: Low response rate on cold outreach (1--2%), but very high value per conversion. One corporate contact can generate multiple transactions. Focus on building relationships rather than volume mailers.

## 14. Legal & Ethical Framework

### Overarching Legal Requirements

| Regulation | Applies To | Key Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| **Fair Housing Act** | All outreach | Cannot target or exclude based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status, disability |
| **Do Not Call Registry (FTC)** | Phone outreach | Must scrub phone lists against the National DNC Registry. Exemption exists for existing business relationships |
| **CAN-SPAM Act** | Email outreach | Must include opt-out mechanism, physical address, accurate subject lines |
| **State telemarketing laws** | Phone outreach | Many states have stricter calling restrictions than federal law |
| **TCPA (Telephone Consumer Protection Act)** | Phone/text | Written consent required for autodialed calls or texts to cell phones |
| **State real estate licensing laws** | All marketing | Must identify yourself as a licensed real estate agent in all marketing |
| **RESPA (Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act)** | Referral relationships | Cannot pay or receive referral fees to/from non-licensees in most cases |
| **State foreclosure protection statutes** | Pre-foreclosure outreach | Many states restrict contact with homeowners in foreclosure |
| **GDPR/CCPA** | Data handling | California and other jurisdictions have data privacy laws affecting how you store and use personal information |

### Ethical Best Practices

1. **Transparency**: Always identify yourself as a real estate agent in all communications. Never misrepresent your purpose.
2. **Empathy first**: Many of these leads involve people in difficult situations. Lead with how you can help, not with what you want to gain.
3. **No deception**: Never imply that you have special information, government authority, or that the homeowner is in worse shape than they are.
4. **Opt-out respect**: If someone asks you to stop contacting them, stop immediately and permanently. Document the request.
5. **Fair value**: Always recommend listing at fair market value. Never steer a distressed homeowner toward a below-market sale for your or an investor's benefit.
6. **Disclosure**: Disclose any investor relationships, dual agency situations, or financial interests beyond your commission.
7. **Record keeping**: Maintain records of all outreach, responses, and opt-out requests for compliance purposes.

### Compliance Checklist Before Launching Any Campaign

- [ ] Marketing materials reviewed by broker for compliance
- [ ] Phone lists scrubbed against National and State DNC registries
- [ ] Agent identified as licensed Realtor in all materials
- [ ] No references to protected class characteristics in targeting
- [ ] Opt-out mechanism included in all communications
- [ ] State-specific foreclosure contact restrictions reviewed
- [ ] Data storage practices comply with applicable privacy laws
- [ ] No exterior envelope/postcard references to foreclosure, divorce, or death
